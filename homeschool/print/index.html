Tip 1. Government Involvement in Homeschooling

In the U.S., thus in Minnesota, homeschooling still involves the government. As citizens of the United States and Minnesota, we husbands and fathers agree to abide by the laws written by the men of the U.S. and Minnesota government. Regarding education, the U.S. federal government sets only broad laws. To receive federal education funding, a state must follow federal education laws. For more on that dynamic, read the Congressional Research Service's (CRS) Summary of Federal Education Laws Administered by the U.S. Department of Education (2022 update). (The U.S. Congress directs the CRS, which is a nonpartisan entity tasked with conducting research to help Congress.) Again, the extent of those federal laws is to offer federal money to states for following certain basic laws of the U.S. Department of Education, which is federal.

Meanwhile, nearly all government involvement in education arises under state law. Thus, as Minnesota fathers and husbands, we are concerned with the Minnesota Education Code, which Minnesota law defines as chapters 120A to 129C of the Minnesota statutes. Minn. Stat. 120A.01 ("Chapters 120A to 129C may be cited as the Education Code.").

Beyond that, each local school district must follow its state's laws. A district may set its own local policies, as long as the local policies do not conflict with state law about education or civil rights. (Nor may the local policies conflict with federal laws, for example federal civil rights laws.)

Learn more about the Minnesota Education Code below, at Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Education Code > 120A.22 (Compulsory Instruction). There, you will find a link to a PDF of the Minnesota Education Code. I personally edited the code for clarity and relevance to conservative Christian husbands who are helping their wives to homeschool their children, or who are interested in starting. The PDF also has a link to the full, unedited Minnesota Education Code at the government's website.

Tip 2. Yearly Testing of Homeschool Kids

*For information on getting free test materials from your district, see Tip 3 below. Also, the Minnesota Association of Christian Home Educators (MACHE) has some information about finding national standardized tests. MACHE may be reached with the information below: Links > Minnesota Homeschool Resources > MACHE.

Yearly Testing: General Rule. When your wife homeschools your kid, Minnesota law will generally require yearly testing of your kid: "a nationally norm-referenced standarized achievement examination." Minn. Stat. § 120A.22, subd.11(a).

Exceptions. Sub-section (b) of the above law provides exceptions to the general testing rule if your wife, or another who teaches your homeschooled child, meets one of the following three criteria: (1) has a Minnesota teaching license for the subject and grade-level taught; or (2) is directly supervised by a person with a Minnesota teaching license; or (3) instructs in a school accredited by an accrediting agency recognize by section 123B.445.

Read the laws cited about by going to PDF below at Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Education Code.

Tip 3. Free Textbooks, Tests, and Software

You local district has a duty under law to honor your request if you ask for certain resources to homeschool your child. For more about that, check the sources below at Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Statute 123b (school district aid to nonpublic students) > 123B.42 (Textbooks; Individual Instruction or Cooperative Learning Material; Standard Tests).

Here is an example of the the laws.

123B.42 Textbooks; Individual Instruction or Cooperative Learning Material; Standard Tests.

Subdivision 1. District Providing Free Education Materials and Tests for Homeschooled Kids. The commissioner of education shall promulgate rules under the provisions of chapter 14 requiring that in each school year, based upon formal requests by or on behalf of nonpublic school pupils in a nonpublic school, the local districts or intermediary service areas must purchase or otherwise acquire textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, software or other educational technology, and standardized tests and loan or provide them for use by children enrolled in that nonpublic school. These textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, software or other educational technology, and standardized tests must be loaned or provided free to the children for the school year for which requested. The loan or provision of the textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, and standardized tests shall be subject to rules prescribed by the commissioner of education.

Subd. 1a. District Providing Free Electronic and Software parts of Curriculum to Homeschooled Kids. A school district that provides curriculum to resident students that has both physical and electronic components must make the electronic component accessible to a resident student in a home school in compliance with sections 120A.22 and 120A.24 at the request of the student or the student's parent or guardian, provided that the district does not incur more than an incidental cost as a result of providing access electronically.

Step 3. Evaluate your wife's desire

As a husband and father, you obviously have authority over your wife, and your role is to guide her. However, be fair - and realistic - about whether your wife really wants to homeschool.

Step 4. Evaluate your wife's ability

Again, be realistic. Even if your wife is motiavted to homeschool, still, homeschooling could be beyond her ability and might overwhelm her. Consider allowing her to try homeschooling - and know that you can change back to non-homeschooling if homeschool turns out to be beyond your wife's ability for now. (And obviously consider letting her try again, even soon after failure, if she thinks, and you agree, that she may have improved her ability to homeschool.)

Step 5. Keep your kid home; notify the district

Homeschooling just means legally keeping your kid home and teaching them there (and around town, as needed or wanted). Part of legally keeping your kid home is notifying the district that you are invoking your right under law to homeschool. If you withdraw your kid during the school year, Minnesota law gives you 15 days to notify the district that you have withdrawn your kid. Minn. Stats. § 120A.24, subd.1(2). You have longer if you withdraw before the school year has started. A PDF of those laws is below: Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Education Code.

Step 6. Find curricula

*As indicated in Tip 3 above: as a homeschooling family, you can get free textbooks, tests, and other resources from the district, every year, to help you meet the curricular requirements described below.

Minnesota law requires the teaching of these subjects: (1) basic communication skills including reading and writing, literature, and fine arts; (2) mathematics and science; (3) social studies including history, geography, economics, government, and citizenship; and (4) health and physical education. Minn. Stat. § Subd.9. A PDF of this law is below: Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Education Code. Minnesota law does not define how to teach these subjects, nor does the law define grade-levels for the teaching. Still, the safest decision when dealing with laws is to act in good faith and be reasonable. Generally, when judging a vague standard, courts typically settle on determining whether conduct was reasonable.

Curricula are everywhere for homeschooling. Indeed, simple guidance from a parent on basic living may qualify to meet certain standards. For example, the health education standard may be met by a parent talking to their child about the importance of healthy eating and giving examples. Homeschooling can involve a lot of creativity.

If you want your wife to teach your child from ready-made texts, many companies offer that. Many homeschool expenses will qualify for a tax credit. To learn about that, visit the Minnesota Department of Revenue's webpage about the K–12 Education Subtraction and Credit. The Department also describes the Qualifying Home School Expenses for K-12 Education Subtraction and Credit

Step 7. Find a qualified teacher or teachers

Under Minnesota law, your wife, as your kid mother, is a qualified teacher. See Minn. Stat. § 120A.24, subd.10(5). Otherwise, a teacher qualified under law meets one or more of the following standards: (1) holds a valid Minnesota teaching license in the field and for the grade level taught; (2) is directly supervised by a person holding a valid Minnesota teaching license; (3) provides instruction in a school that is accredited by an accrediting agency, recognized according to section 123B.445, or recognized by the commissioner; or (4) has a bachelor's degree. Minn. Stat. § 120A.24, subd.10. A PDF of these laws is below: Links > Minnesota Education Laws for Homeschool > Minnesota Education Code.
