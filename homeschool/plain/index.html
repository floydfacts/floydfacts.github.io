The first and best government contact for homeschooling is generally the superintendent's office for your kid's district. If you are unsure about how to contact that office, feel free to call your local school. Your local school can tell you how to contact the superintendent. 2.1. Clarify your motivations

The first step to start homeschooling is to understand why you want your wife to homeschool your children, instead of having the government as your children's teacher - and then to help her understand why.

2.2. Clarify your goals

After determining why you want your wife to homeschool your children, next and clarify, for yourself and your wife, the goals that you have for homeschool. Consider writing a list.

2.3. Evaluate your wife's desire

As a husband and father, you obviously have authority over your wife, and your role is to guide her. Moreover, the best exercise of your authority is to be fair and realistic about whether your wife really wants to homeschool.

2.4. Evaluate your wife's ability

Again, your best strategy here is to be fair and realistic. Even if your wife is motivated to homeschool, still, homeschooling could be beyond her ability and might overwhelm her. Consider allowing her to try homeschooling - and know that you can change back to non-homeschooling if homeschool turns out to be beyond your wife's ability for now. (And obviously consider letting her try again, even soon after failure, if she thinks, and you agree, that she may have improved her ability to homeschool.)

2.5. Keep your kid home and notify the district

Homeschooling just means legally keeping your kid home and teaching them there (and around town, as needed or wanted). Part of legally keeping your kid home is notifying the district that you are invoking your right under law to homeschool. If you withdraw your kid during the school year, Minnesota law gives you 15 days to notify the district that you have withdrawn your kid. Minn. Stats. § 120A.24, subd.1(2). You have longer if you withdraw before the school year has started. A PDF of those laws is below: 7. Government > Minnesota Homeschool Law > Minnesota Education Code.

The Forms section below has the forms needed to legally notify your school district that you will be homeschooling. Check Mache at 6.1 for free info on which forms to send and when.

2.6. Find a qualified teacher or teachers

Under Minnesota law, your wife, as your kid mother, is a qualified teacher. See Minn. Stat. § 120A.24, subd.10(5). Otherwise, a teacher qualified under law meets one or more of the following standards:

  • holds a valid Minnesota teaching license in the field and for the grade level taught;
  • is directly supervised by a person holding a valid Minnesota teaching license;
  • provides instruction in a school that is accredited by an accrediting agency, recognized according to section 123B.445, or recognized by the commissioner; or
  • has a bachelor's degree. Minn. Stat. § 120A.24, subd.10. A PDF of those laws is below: 7. Government > Minnesota Homeschool Law > Minnesota Education Code.

3.2. Yearly Testing of Homeschool Kids

*For information on getting free test materials from your district, see Tip 3 below. Also, the Minnesota Association of Christian Home Educators (MACHE) has some information about finding national standardized tests. MACHE may be reached with the information below: Minnesota XHS (Christian Homeschool) Groups > MACHE.

Yearly Testing, General Rule: When your wife homeschools your kid, Minnesota law will generally require yearly testing of your kid: "a nationally norm-referenced standardized achievement examination." Minn. Stat. § 120A.22, subd.11(a).

Exceptions. Sub-section (b) of the above law exemmpts you from the general testing rule if your wife, or another who teaches your homeschooled child, meets one of the following three criteria:

  1. has a Minnesota teaching license for the subject and grade-level taught; or
  2. is directly supervised by a person with a Minnesota teaching license; or
  3. instructs in a school accredited by an accrediting agency recognized by (a) the commissioner of the Minnesota Department of Education, or (b) the nonpublic education council, under section 123B.445 of the Minnesota Education Code (available at 7.2.1 below).

3.3. Free Textbooks, Tests, and Software

You local district has a duty under law to honor your request if you ask for certain resources to homeschool your child. For more about that, check the sources below at 7.2.2. > 123B.42 (Textbooks; Individual Instruction or Cooperative Learning Material; Standard Tests).

Here is an example of the the laws.

123B.42 Textbooks; Individual Instruction or Cooperative Learning Material; Standard Tests.

Subdivision 1. District Providing Free Education Materials and Tests for Homeschooled Kids. The commissioner of education shall promulgate rules under the provisions of chapter 14 requiring that in each school year, based upon formal requests by or on behalf of nonpublic school pupils in a nonpublic school, the local districts or intermediary service areas must purchase or otherwise acquire textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, software or other educational technology, and standardized tests and loan or provide them for use by children enrolled in that nonpublic school. These textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, software or other educational technology, and standardized tests must be loaned or provided free to the children for the school year for which requested. The loan or provision of the textbooks, individualized instructional or cooperative learning materials, and standardized tests shall be subject to rules prescribed by the commissioner of education.

Subd. 1a. District Providing Free Electronic and Software parts of Curriculum to Homeschooled Kids. A school district that provides curriculum to resident students that has both physical and electronic components must make the electronic component accessible to a resident student in a home school in compliance with sections 120A.22 and 120A.24 at the request of the student or the student's parent or guardian, provided that the district does not incur more than an incidental cost as a result of providing access electronically.

3.8. Early Graduation or Exclusion

Minnesota law exempts from compulsory education any student (homeschooled or otherwise) who either (a) finishes the eighth grade, or else (b) graduates from high school (and for a homeschooled student, high school graduation arises by whatever criteria the parent sets). Legal analysis of these points follows.

Minnesota law exempts graduated students from compulsory education. Minn. Stat. 120A.22, subd.5(a) (exempting graduated children from compulsory instruction). Generally, a student may be graduated as soon as they complete the necessary work: "Any [high school] student who has completed all required courses or standards may, with the approval of the student, the student's parent or guardian, and local school officials, graduate before the completion of the school year." Minn. Stat. 120B.07.

In any case, state law cannot compel education beyond the 8th grade. Wisconsin v. Yoder, 406 U.S. 205 (1972); cf. Minnesota Department of Education, Home School Education: Home-School Questions and Answers (2023), https://education.mn.gov/mde/fam/nphs/home (stating that students have "a right to discontinue education, reporting and testing following eighth grade" (echoing the Yoder decision)).

A child may pass the eighth grade by passing a lawful eighth-grade test. The Minnesota Department of Education lists some lawful tests. University of Minnesota, Welcome to the Minnesota Statewide Testing Program Store (n.d.), https://mstp-umn.nbsstore.net. A child passes a grade-level test by scoring above the 30th percentile. Minn. Stat. 120A.22 Subd.11(c) (2023) (mandating remediation only if a child's performance on the test "is at or below the 30th percentile").
